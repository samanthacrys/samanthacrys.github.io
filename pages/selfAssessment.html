Journey and Education at SNHU

In my time at SNHU, I have seen many types of programming. Before all of this, I had done some work with companies that used game development engines such as Unity and Godot. The thing was, I began my journey at SNHU to take my experience with these companies and the experience I have as a technical writer a step further. I had noticed that without a degree in computer science, it was harder for me to get work in the technical writing field. This led me to choosing to gain my degree in computer science to expand my knowledge and make it easier to get a job.

During my time here, I have learned many different topics. Some of those topics were ones that I had some knowledge and experience in from start and others I did not. One of the most interesting experiences for me was taking CS-330. This was one of the more difficult classes. This class was where I worked with rendering an image. I had to take an image that I created and, over the weeks, build the image up to create the final product. This left me with many different qualities I had to work with, especially with some of the objects I rendered being connected to a commercial product. This led me to having to make decisions on what I could do and what was okay to do. I ended up choosing to go with a base metal finish in colors close enough to the original object to avoid copyright and trademark issues. This was mainly due to my previous background as a writer and author. Those fields require me to understand copywrite law. The main thing, however, was that I found the class interesting, especially with choosing to go through with a software engineering concentration. Generally speaking, I was used to the development engines I worked with for the rendering. To learn the behind-the-scenes architecture gave me more knowledge.

That knowledge prepared me when looking at data structures and algorithms throughout the program. To be able to understand the base architecture allowed me to better understand the data and the algorithms that went into everything. It’s simple to go into something and do what everyone has done before you, but to have better knowledge in the actual details and structure helps for improving the overall thinking that a programmer needs. The biggest thing I had seen was the ability to problem solve and the more knowledge that I had, the better I was able to do that. I understand how data is transferred, stored, and the algorithms needed to retrieve it, sort it, and so on.

My Skills

Not that this knowledge was everything that I gained. I had to understand working with a team, communicating information to the clients, knowing what security was needed, and so on. At times, some of these were difficult, as working in an online environment can be very isolating and harder to gain feedback on improvement. I took that challenge and welcomed it with open-arms.

The use of my background as an author has given me an advantage when it comes to communicating with clients. I have taken my past experience as a technical writer with that author connection to understand how to ask questions of the clients to figure out what they need and to explain it simply. I am capable of taking complex and technical topics and breaking them down. I can take that breakdown and put it into simple terms that are understandable to those who do not have the technical knowledge. I have a strong ability to research so that the understanding may be presented in an understandable way without forcing knowledge that they may not want or need.

I can even apply that when working in teams. I have worked in teams many times over the years and I have collaborated on many projects. Often times I’m the organizer that takes all the information and organizes it in a way that allows everyone to know what has been done, what needs to be done, and who feels comfortable with what. I discuss with my team, make sure everything I’m doing is done on time, and help others when they ask for it. This does include knowing who may be the best to help if it is not me.

I have also worked with reverse engineering and databases as well. The reverse engineering allows me to take a finished product and go backwards to break it down and understand what was done and where it’s coming from. That lets me find holes in the security and the system that can be improved and or fixed. This ties into databases as I have worked in SQL Server, MongoDB, and MySQL. All three of these databases are used as ways to create a storage system for the system. The data within needs protecting and I have directly worked with security to search for holes, research the solutions, and implement those solutions to allow for the security of the data structure to remain up-to-date.

Portfolio

In terms of my portfolio, I feel that it flows together and shows my capabilities. The first artifact was for software design and engineering. This artifact was close to me and showed my ability to create and design a finished product that was playable, but also written in a common language. This was a game that I did in IT-140. This game was a simple text-adventure. I had initially planned a major story that the player would engage in when writing the original and this portfolio piece allowed me to implement my story and improve upon it in a more interactive-fiction style. Since I am an author in trade, I feel this showcases my ability to complete a larger project that ties in creative elements with coding and following best practices. That is important as, even when doing technical writing, you need to be able to follow a project from beginning to completion. Not being able to do that can bring doubt into one’s capabilities. I wanted to show that I could follow throw and bring that project to completion in the way I intended and made sense for the structure of the story.

My next artifact was that of a data structure and algorithm. I chose to work with my task service class from CS-320. The reason I chose this project and how it flows into the previous one is that it related to converting the code from an older version of Java to a current version of C#. I had chosen this because I have experience with the C# library, but also for security reasons. The older version of Java used wasn’t as secure through being both older and not getting updated. I used a modern version of C# that is currently being updated to ensure that my code would remain secure and easily fixable when security holes were found. I feel that it shows I can create a program that can test to ensure that everything works through unit testing, but also that I can keep a security mindset.

The last artifact was for databases. I chose my CS-340 final project. This was a dashboard that connected to a mongoDB. Throughout everything I did, I had some form of database. The text-adventure had a list of objects and what they were capable of and my task class had a dictionary of tasks. These are all a type of database, but this one I had a dashboard that was web-based connecting to one. It was originally written in python, which was fine, and I did keep it that way, but it wasn’t the most secure product. While the python itself wasn’t the problem, the code had hard-coded username and password within the file. This is against best practices and I knew I needed to implement a method that would allow me to secure the system. At first, I looked at it more as improving the querying speed, but the amount of data was so small that this wasn’t necessary and I noticed that, with my other projects, I needed to look at it more in a what is best for the program itself. In this case, that was increasing the security to bring the program to completion. I knew that it wouldn’t be a good thing to leave in that hard-coded information. I was able to take the knowledge I had learned in a different class, CS-405 secure coding, and apply it here to make the web application more secure for connecting to the database.
